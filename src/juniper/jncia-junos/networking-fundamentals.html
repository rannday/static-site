This section is the baseline: know how Ethernet forwarding, IP addressing, and simple route selection work before tying them back to Junos behavior. For JNCIA-Junos, definitions and basic calculations matter more than edge cases.

What to know

  • A collision domain is where Ethernet devices can interfere with each other; a switch breaks collision domains per port, while a broadcast domain normally ends at a router.
  • Switches primarily forward frames at Layer 2 by MAC address. Routers forward packets at Layer 3 by IP prefix and do not forward broadcasts by default.
  • Ethernet uses MAC addressing on the local segment. ARP resolves IPv4 addresses to MAC addresses, and IPv6 uses Neighbor Discovery for the same basic purpose.
  • Subnet masks and prefix lengths define network and host bits. Subnetting creates smaller networks; supernetting aggregates routes into larger summaries.
  • IPv4 is 32-bit addressing and IPv6 is 128-bit addressing. Junos supports both, so be comfortable reading both prefix formats.
  • Binary conversion shows up in subnetting questions. You should be able to convert common decimal octets to binary and back.
  • Longest-prefix match wins during forwarding. The most specific matching route is selected over a broader route.
  • Class of Service (CoS) is traffic classification and treatment. At this level, understand that traffic can be marked and queued differently.
  • Connection-oriented protocols build and track a session. Connectionless protocols send traffic without first establishing one.

Quick checks

  • If a packet matches both `10.0.0.0/8` and `10.1.1.0/24`, the `/24` wins.
  • Routers separate broadcast domains. Switches expand a LAN but do not stop Layer 2 broadcasts on their own.
  • ARP is for IPv4. IPv6 uses Neighbor Discovery instead of ARP.
